SCAM 1: PIG BUTCHERING (SHA ZHU PAN)
Entry: Wrong number/accidental contact on WhatsApp, Telegram, or dating apps
7 Stages: Contact → Perfect Persona → Emotional Bond → Financial Pivot → Platform Introduction → Small Deposit → Trap/Collapse
Global losses: $12.4 billion annually. UK average loss: £18,500 per victim.
Profile: Usually presents as East Asian (Korean/Chinese/Japanese), highly successful career, luxury lifestyle (Bentley, penthouse, private jet), often widowed or divorced for sympathy
Crypto transfers always preferred — untraceable and irreversible
Key red flags: Fast bonding <3 days, avoids live video calls, asks about capital, "aunt/mentor/uncle taught me to trade", pictures too perfect (AI-generated)
Platform: ALWAYS extract dynamically from the chat — NEVER assume. Could be UniSat, Binance, anything.
95-day average relationship before victim realises (TSB Bank data 2025)
Peak seasons: February-March (Valentine's), September (students return to university)
Operated from scam compounds in Cambodia, Myanmar, Philippines — often using trafficked workers

SCAM 4: RECOVERY SCAMS (Double Victimisation — Critical Warning)
Previous fraud victims specifically purchased from dark web and targeted again
Pose as FCA, HMRC, police, UK courts, law firms, or specialist "crypto recovery agencies"
FBI documented pattern: Fictitious law firms impersonating real solicitors, referencing exact amounts previously lost, placing victims in WhatsApp groups for "secrecy", requesting upfront fees in crypto or gift cards
SPECIFIC SCAM IDENTIFIED: vkiv5.cc/v2n5dxm — claiming to be "Legal crypto asset refund" overseen by "High Court of Justice UK & EEA" = CONFIRMED SCAM. UK government websites ALWAYS end in .gov.uk. This domain is not government.
Red flags of ANY recovery scam:
  - Any upfront fee before "releasing" funds
  - Non-.gov.uk domain claiming to be UK government
  - "Guaranteed" recovery
  - Communication via Gmail/Yahoo not corporate email
  - Requests for crypto payment or gift cards
  - Knowledge of exact amounts previously lost (bought from scammer)
  - WhatsApp group for "secrecy"
  - References to fictitious agencies (e.g., "International Financial Trading Commission")
Legitimate recovery only via: Action Fraud 0300 123 2040, FCA (fca.org.uk/scamsmart), Financial Ombudsman 0800 023 4567, Citizens Advice 0808 223 1133

SCAM 6: WHATSAPP OTP HIJACKING (Most Widespread UK Scam 2026)
The attack sequence:
1. Hacker enters victim's number in WhatsApp on their device
2. WhatsApp sends 6-digit verification code to victim's phone
3. Hacker contacts victim — posing as friend/family/WhatsApp Support/"prize team"
4. Claims the code was sent by mistake or that victim has won a prize
5. Victim shares code — hacker instantly owns the WhatsApp account
6. Victim locked out, hacker impersonates them to contacts, solicits emergency money transfers
7. Hacker spreads same attack to victim's entire contact list
Common deception scripts scammers use:
  "Sorry, I accidentally sent a code to your number — can you forward it?"
  "You've won a competition, send us the verification code to claim your prize"
  "Hi Mum, I've lost my phone, can you send £200 urgently to this number?"
  "WhatsApp Support: your account needs verification, please provide the code sent to you"
NEW 2026 THREAT — GhostPairing Attack: Victim receives message from apparent contact with Facebook-style preview link. Victim clicks, completes "verification" steps that secretly add attacker's browser as a linked device. Attacker gets SILENT access to all conversations WITHOUT locking victim out. Victim has no idea they're being monitored.
Protection: Enable Two-Step Verification in WhatsApp Settings → Account (single most effective defence)
ABSOLUTE RULE: NEVER share a WhatsApp verification code — not with friends, family, or "support"

SCAM 7: ANDROID MALWARE (2026 Active Threats)
Albiriox: Most dangerous active threat. Full Android Remote Access Trojan. Criminals stream victim's screen LIVE and perform banking transactions directly on the phone in real-time while the victim watches a black screen. Targets 400+ banking, fintech, payment and crypto apps. Spread via fake apps and smishing messages.
PromptSpy: World's first Android malware using Google's Gemini AI to guide its own attacks. Blocks uninstallation via invisible overlays. Continuously captures lockscreen and device data silently.
Keenadu: Pre-installed backdoor in budget phone/tablet firmware — present before device switched on. Injects into every app. Can be bought on sites pretending to sell cheap electronics.
BeatBanker: Disguised as a Starlink app distributed via fake Google Play sites. Banking credential theft combined with hidden cryptocurrency miner draining battery and data silently.
Vapor: 60 million Google Play installs as utility apps (QR scanners, wallpaper apps, expense trackers). Unskippable ads plus hides itself to prevent removal.
WhatsApp-spread malware: Malicious APK files sent in WhatsApp messages with enticing filenames. Once installed, auto-spreads to all contacts. Primary delivery mechanism for multiple malware families in 2026.
CRITICAL RED FLAG: Any app requesting Accessibility Services permission that isn't an official screen reader. Legitimate apps almost never need this — virtually ALL banking malware abuses it to take over device completely.
Protection rules:
  - Only install apps from official Google Play Store or Apple App Store
  - Enable Google Play Protect on Android (Settings → Security → Google Play Protect)
  - NEVER install APK files from WhatsApp messages, texts, or emails
  - Deny Accessibility Services access to any app you don't trust completely
  - Review all app permissions regularly — remove access that seems excessive

SCAM 14: DELIVERY DRIVER / RIDER SCAMS (Uber Eats, Deliveroo, DoorDash, Amazon Flex, Just Eat)
Scale: Widespread black market for rented/sold gig accounts via Facebook groups. 171 delivery riders arrested in single UK Home Office enforcement blitz (November 2025).
Fake Platform Support Calls — most common:
  - Scammer creates fake customer order → places it → calls driver through Uber app (shows official caller ID)
  - Claims there's a "serious account issue" or "complaint" requiring urgent "verification"
  - Asks for verification code, login credentials, or bank details
  - Once obtained: reroutes ALL driver earnings to scammer's account
  - Driver doesn't realise until payday — weeks of earnings stolen
  - RULE: Uber, Deliveroo, DoorDash, Just Eat, Amazon Flex NEVER call asking for passwords or verification codes
"Food Never Arrived" Customer Scam:
  - False claims food was not delivered to get free refund
  - Repeated false complaints trigger platform deactivation — driver loses entire income stream
E-Bike Theft Lure:
  - Criminals place fake orders to lure riders to isolated or poorly lit locations
  - Accomplices steal e-bike while driver is completing delivery
  - E-bikes cost £1,500–£5,000. Usually not insured adequately.
Fake Third-Party Apps:
  - Apps promising route optimisation, order boosting, or higher earnings
  - Advertised in driver Facebook groups and WhatsApp communities — high trust environment
  - Actually steal login credentials to platform accounts
  - Delivered as APK files or links to fake app stores
Overtip Lure Scam:
  - Suspiciously high tips as bait (£30+ on a £15 order)
  - Either tip removed after delivery, or order leads driver to dangerous/isolated location
Account Sharing Black Market:
  - Legitimate riders rent/sell verified accounts on Facebook groups (as low as $65)
  - Used by those without right to work, or with criminal records preventing platform approval
  - Renting your account = account deactivation + potential legal liability under immigration law
  - New Border Security, Asylum and Immigration Act 2025: employers face £60,000 fine per illegal worker

SCAM 15 / RISK: DATA BREACH AWARENESS
UK GDPR key facts:
  - Accidental breach is still a breach regardless of intent
  - Uploading confidential company data (PII-labelled) to ANY third-party AI platform = potential breach
  - Example: Uploading a Vodafone/employer dashboard screenshot with business KPIs to an AI tool
  - 72-Hour Rule: Must report to ICO within 72 hours if breach poses risk to individuals
  - ICO fined Capita £14 million in October 2025 — largest ever fine — for exposing 6.6 million people's data
  - Fines: up to £17.5 million OR 4% of global annual turnover (whichever higher)
  - Data (Use and Access) Act 2025 received Royal Assent June 2025 — updated compliance requirements
Mitigating factors if accidental breach occurs:
  - No individually identifiable personal data visible: lower risk
  - Self-reporting immediately: always viewed more favourably
  - Quick containment: reduces severity assessment
What to do after accidental corporate data upload to AI:
  1. Tell your manager or Data Protection Officer immediately
  2. Document what data was shared, when, and where
  3. Assess whether individual personal data was included
  4. ICO contact: ico.org.uk | 0303 123 1113
  5. Self-reporting protects you legally and professionally

SCREENSHOT INTELLIGENCE LAYER — VISUAL SCAM PATTERN RECOGNITION 2026

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
PATTERN 1: FAKE CRYPTO TRADING DASHBOARD
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

What it looks like:
- Sleek dark-themed interface mimicking Binance, Coinbase, or OKX
- Shows inflated profit figures: "+£18,500 today" or "+340% this week"
- Has a large green "Total Balance" or "Profit" number prominently displayed
- Withdrawal button is missing, greyed out, or triggers "Tax Payment Required" popup
- Shows "VIP Member" or "Tier 3 Investor" status badges
- Fake real-time candlestick charts with upward-only movement
- "Customer Support" chat widget that is actually the scammer
- Referral codes, invite links, and "bonus" counters

Why it's fake:
- No FCA registration number displayed anywhere
- Domain registered within last 3-6 months (check WHOIS)
- URL slightly wrong: "binance-pro.com" instead of "binance.com"
- Profits only visible on screen — no withdrawals ever processed
- Withdrawal always blocked by a new fee: tax, verification, insurance, "security deposit"

ScamShield response: Score 95+. Fake trading platform. Part of pig butchering or investment scam.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
PATTERN 2: GLAMOROUS LIFESTYLE PHOTOS FROM SCAMMER
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

What it looks like:
- Professional-quality photos of attractive person
- Luxury cars (Bentley, Ferrari, Range Rover), penthouses, yachts
- Expensive restaurants, designer goods, business class airport lounges
- Photos always perfectly lit, professional composition — not like normal phone selfies
- Multiple locations shown: Hong Kong, Singapore, Dubai, Manchester

Red flags in the images:
- Reverse image search often finds the photos belong to a model or influencer
- Photos are AI-generated (no shadows match, background blurs wrong)
- Person never appears in video call — always "bad connection" or "camera broken"
- Lifestyle inconsistent with claimed profession (e.g. "nurse in Manchester" with yacht)

ScamShield response: Flag as romance/pig butchering scam identity construction.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
PATTERN 3: FAKE PROFIT SCREENSHOTS SENT BY SCAMMER
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

What it looks like:
- Screenshots of a wallet showing £50,000, £100,000, or more
- "Withdrawal Confirmed" receipts that look like bank transfer confirmations
- Group chats showing other "investors" celebrating profits: "I just withdrew £8,000!"
- Fake Coinbase/Binance balance screenshots edited to show huge numbers
- Side-by-side before/after screenshots: "£500 became £14,000 in 3 days!"

How to spot a fake:
- Font rendering slightly off or inconsistent in the numbers
- Timestamps don't match timezone claimed
- All "investors" in group chat use identical phrasing and perfect English
- Group members have similar profile photos — all attractive, successful-looking

ScamShield response: Score 97+. These screenshots are fabricated. Real profits cannot be verified from screenshots.

STAGE 2 — FINDING ACCOMMODATION (Week 1 — HIGHEST RISK PERIOD)

STAGE 3 — VISA & IMMIGRATION SCAMS (Ongoing — Highest Fear Factor)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

SCAM: UKVI / HOME OFFICE IMPERSONATION CALL
The single most reported scam against international students in UK.
Script: Caller claims to be UKVI officer. Already has student's
name, passport number, UK postcode — making it terrifyingly real.
Tells student there is a "serious problem with your visa" and they
must pay a fine immediately by MoneyGram, Western Union, iTunes
gift cards, cryptocurrency, or wire transfer to avoid deportation.
If student hesitates, caller becomes aggressive, threatening arrest.

UKVI WILL NEVER:
- Call you and ask for money
- Ask for payment by phone, email, bank transfer
- Request payment via gift cards, cryptocurrency, or MoneyGram
- Threaten immediate arrest or deportation by phone
- Ask you to keep the call secret from your university

What to say: "I know this is a scam targeting international
students. I will not be paying anything. I am reporting this call."
Then hang up immediately.
Report to: Action Fraud 0300 123 2040 and your university
international office.

SCAM: FAKE VISA AGENTS / CERTIFICATE OF SPONSORSHIP FRAUD
Unlicensed agents promise to arrange visas or work sponsorships

savings, too afraid to return home or report it.
Check: Only use OISC-registered advisers. Verify at gov.uk.

STAGE 4 — VIRTUAL KIDNAPPING (Chinese & East Asian Students)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

One of the most psychologically devastating scams in existence.
Specifically targets Chinese, Taiwanese, and East Asian students
studying abroad in the UK, US, Australia, Canada.

HOW IT WORKS — STEP BY STEP:
1. Student receives call in Mandarin from someone claiming to be
   Chinese police / Public Security Bureau / Prosecutor's Office
2. Told they are implicated in a serious crime in China
   (money laundering, fraud, immigration abuse)
3. Ordered to keep call completely secret — "tell no one"
4. Ordered to isolate themselves: check into hotel room alone,
   cut off ALL contact with family and friends
5. Told to photograph/video themselves bound, blindfolded, or
   in a vulnerable position and send to scammers
6. Scammers simultaneously call the student's PARENTS IN CHINA
   claiming their child has been kidnapped
7. Parents shown the photos/videos as "proof"
8. Ransom demanded: £20,000–£500,000+
9. Parents pay. Scammers disappear. Student is found alone,
   terrified, and traumatised.

WHY VICTIMS COMPLY: Deep cultural fear of Chinese police,
concern that family in China could be harmed, being alone
and isolated in a foreign country, language barrier preventing
them from seeking help from UK authorities.

AI EVOLUTION 2026: Scammers now use AI voice cloning to
generate fake "distress calls" from the student to their parents
in real time, making the scam even more convincing.

WHAT TO DO:
- Hang up immediately
- Call your parents directly on a SEPARATE phone/channel
- Contact your university international office
- Call UK police 999 if you feel in immediate danger
- Chinese/Taiwanese students: contact your embassy/consulate

STAGE 5 — PHYSICAL SCAMS ON THE STREET & HIGH STREET
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

These scams happen daily in UK city centres. New students
don't know what's normal and what's a scam.

SCAM: PICKPOCKETING — DISTRACTION TECHNIQUE
Hotspots: Oxford Street, Leicester Square, Covent Garden,
Camden Market, London Underground (Victoria, King's Cross,
Oxford Circus, Stratford stations — 4,000+ victims/year)
Method: Works in teams. One distracts (asks for directions,
drops something, bumps into you, shows you a map, asks you
to sign something). Accomplice empties pocket/bag.
Advanced version: "Bag cutters" slice open bottom of bag
and catch contents in their own bag.
Protection: Bag on chest/front, zip everything, never put
phone/wallet in back pocket, hand on valuables in crowds.

SCAM: MOPED / E-BIKE PHONE SNATCHING EPIDEMIC
80,000 phones stolen in London in 2024. Organised gangs on
e-bikes or mopeds patrol Oxford Street, Shoreditch, Westminster.
Snatch phone from hand while you are standing or walking.
Over in under 3 seconds. Phones exported to China via criminal networks.
Protection: Never hold phone visibly on busy streets. Use

SCAM: FAKE FLOWER / FREE GIFT
Common at: Trafalgar Square, Covent Garden, tourist areas
Method: Friendly person approaches and places flower in your

you accept, they demand payment. If you refuse, some become
aggressive. Works on new arrivals who don't want to seem rude.

SCAM: CLIPBOARD / PETITION / FAKE CHARITY
Common at: Trafalgar Square, Piccadilly Circus, tube stations

Common at: Leicester Square, tourist areas

People "winning" around you are all accomplices (shills).

Oxford Street and West End areas. Unregulated bicycle rickshaws

SCAM: FAKE QR CODE ON PARKING METERS / SIGNS
Scammers stick fake QR sticker over legitimate payment signs.
When scanned, takes you to professional-looking phishing site

Called "Quishing" (QR + phishing). Growing rapidly across UK.

SCAM: RESTAURANT / BAR HIDDEN CHARGES
Especially in tourist areas. Menu shows one price, bill shows

Protection: Always check menu prices before ordering.

Camden Market, Portobello Road, Westfield, Oxford Street
Fake designer trainers, clothing, perfume, electronics,
vapes sold as genuine. Some contain harmful substances.
Buying counterfeit goods is not illegal for the buyer

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

"DBS check fee". Equipment never arrives, scammer gone.
Rule: Legitimate employers NEVER ask you to pay to start work.

SCAM: FAKE INVESTMENT / FOREX / CRYPTO
Students targeted with "exclusive investment opportunity" via
Instagram or WhatsApp. Often by another student (knowingly
or unknowingly already a victim). Initial returns seem real.
Victim pressured to invest more. Platform freezes. Funds gone.
Students particularly vulnerable as they may have student loan
money sitting in their account.

SCAM: PHISHING FROM FAKE UNIVERSITY SERVICES
Emails mimicking university IT, library, HR, payroll.
Ask for login credentials to "verify your account" or "avoid
deletion". Always go directly to university website — never
click email links claiming to be from your university.

SCAM: FAKE DELIVERY (DHL/Royal Mail/Amazon)
Very common with international students expecting parcels from
home. Text says "customs fee of £2.99 due" with a link.
Link steals card details. Royal Mail never asks for card
payment by text. Forward suspicious texts to 7726.

CRITICAL CONTEXT: AI HAS TRANSFORMED SCAMMING IN 2026
------------------------------------------------------
Artificial intelligence has fundamentally changed how scams look and feel. The old
signs of a scam — poor spelling, bad grammar, obviously fake photos — are now
UNRELIABLE as detection signals. Modern scams are grammatically perfect, highly
personalised, and indistinguishable from genuine communication.

UK fraud reached a record 444,000 cases in 2025 — the highest ever recorded,
averaging over 1,200 incidents every single day. AI is identified as a primary
accelerator. Do NOT rely on poor language quality as a scam indicator.

KEY AI SCAM TYPES NOW ACTIVE IN UK (2026)
-----------------------------------------

1. AI VOICE CLONING SCAMS
   What happens: Scammer clones a family member or friend's voice using as little
   as 3 seconds of audio from social media or voicemail. They call pretending to
   be that person in an emergency needing urgent money.
   Warning signs:
   - Unexpected call from family/friend saying they are in trouble
   - Voice sounds slightly robotic or has unusual pauses
   - Creates extreme urgency — "do not tell anyone else"
   - Asks for money via bank transfer, gift cards or crypto
   - Unable or unwilling to video call to verify identity
   Protection: Agree a secret family code word in advance. Always call the person
   back on their known number before sending any money.

2. AI DEEPFAKE VIDEO SCAMS
   What happens: Scammers create fake videos of celebrities, news anchors or
   financial experts endorsing investment platforms. Often seen on Facebook,
   Instagram, YouTube and TikTok as paid adverts.
   Warning signs:
   - Celebrity promoting a crypto or investment platform in a video ad
   - Face movements look slightly unnatural, lip sync is slightly off
   - High guaranteed returns mentioned
   - Link goes to a professional-looking investment platform
   - Martin Lewis, Elon Musk, Alan Sugar most commonly faked in UK
   Protection: Martin Lewis and other UK celebrities NEVER promote investments in
   video ads. Report to the platform immediately. Check FCA register before investing.

3. AI PHISHING — HYPER-PERSONALISED MESSAGES
   What happens: AI tools scrape your social media, LinkedIn and data breaches to
   craft messages that reference your real name, employer, recent purchases or
   life events. These pass through spam filters and look completely genuine.
   Warning signs:
   - Email or text references specific personal details about you
   - Appears to come from a company you actually use
   - Creates urgency around account security, unpaid bills or deliveries
   - Link URL is slightly different from the real company domain
   Protection: Never click links in emails or texts. Always go directly to the
   company website by typing the address yourself.

4. SCAM-YOURSELF ATTACKS (NEW 2025-2026)
   What happens: Victim is manipulated into installing malware or giving access
   themselves. Common forms include fake CAPTCHA pages, fake browser update
   prompts, fake IT support pop-ups saying device is infected.
   Warning signs:
   - Pop-up saying your computer is infected and to call a number or click a link
   - Fake CAPTCHA that asks you to press Windows+R and paste a command
   - Message saying you need to update your browser immediately
   - Fake QR code in a car park, restaurant or public place
   Protection: No legitimate company sends pop-ups demanding action. Close the
   window and run your real antivirus. Never run commands you did not type yourself.

5. AI-GENERATED FAKE INVESTMENT PLATFORMS
   What happens: Entire professional-looking investment websites, complete with
   fake reviews, fake live trading dashboards and fake profit screenshots are now
   created by AI in minutes. Often promoted via deepfake celebrity video ads.
   Warning signs:
   - Platform not on FCA register at register.fca.org.uk
   - Guaranteed returns or very high returns promised
   - Introduced by someone you met online or through social media
   - Small withdrawals allowed at first to build trust
   - Platform becomes unavailable when you try to withdraw large sums
   Protection: Check register.fca.org.uk before investing a single penny.
   No legitimate UK investment firm will contact you via WhatsApp or social media.

6. SIM SWAP SURGE (UP 38% IN 2025)
   What happens: Criminal calls your mobile network pretending to be you, claims
   to have lost their phone and asks for your number to be moved to a new SIM.
   Once they have your number they intercept all your bank one-time passwords.
   Warning signs:
   - Your phone suddenly has no signal for no apparent reason
   - You receive a text saying your SIM has been activated on a new device
   - You get locked out of banking, email or social media accounts
   - Unusual activity on accounts you have not triggered
   Protection: Call your network immediately if signal drops unexpectedly.
   Enable SIM lock via your network provider. Use an authenticator app
   rather than SMS for two-factor authentication where possible.

7. MALICIOUS BROWSER EXTENSIONS
   What happens: Fake or hijacked browser extensions steal saved passwords,
   session cookies and banking details directly from your browser without
   you knowing.
   Warning signs:
   - Extension asks for permission to read all websites you visit
   - Extension appeared after clicking a link or downloading something
   - You notice unexpected behaviour in your browser
   - Accounts you are logged into show unusual activity
   Protection: Only install browser extensions from the official Chrome or
   Firefox store. Check reviews and permissions carefully. Remove any extensions
   you do not actively use.

UPDATED RISK SCORING GUIDANCE FOR AI SCAMS
-------------------------------------------
When a message is grammatically perfect and highly personalised this is NOW a
WARNING SIGN not a green flag. AI has removed the language quality filter.
Increase risk score if:
- Message references specific personal details (name, employer, recent purchase)
- Message arrives via an unexpected channel (WhatsApp, text, social media DM)
- Message creates urgency around money, security or account access
- Message involves a video, voice call or deepfake element
- Message promotes investment opportunities via social media or messaging apps

--------------------------

- Report deepfake investment ads: FCA at fca.org.uk/consumers/report-scam
- Report fake celebrity ads to the platform (Facebook, YouTube, TikTok)
- Forward scam texts to 7726 (free, all UK networks)
- Forward phishing emails to report@phishing.gov.uk (NCSC)
- SIM swap: call your network provider immediately on their fraud line

12. FINFLUENCER / FAKE TRADING COURSE SCAMS (AGES 13-17)

THREAT LEVEL: HIGH — rapidly growing on TikTok, Instagram Reels, YouTube Shorts

What happens: A young-looking social media account shows a lavish lifestyle —
designer clothes, luxury cars, foreign holidays — claiming it was all funded
through forex trading, crypto, dropshipping or AI trading bots. The account
sells access to a course, a private Discord or Telegram group, or trading
signals, typically priced between 20 and 200 pounds. The content delivered
is worthless, plagiarised or simply a gateway to further upsells.

The UK's Financial Conduct Authority has charged finfluencers for promoting
unauthorised trading schemes on social media. 80% of forex advice found on
TikTok has been assessed as potentially misleading, with only 13% of videos
including any risk disclaimer.

Why under-18s are particularly vulnerable:
- Content looks like financial education or personal development, not fraud
- Young people aspire to the lifestyle shown and want to learn how to achieve it
- Social proof from thousands of followers and comments creates false credibility
- Affordable entry price (20 to 50 pounds) removes financial risk perception

Warning signs:
- "I made 5,000 pounds in a week at 16 with no experience"
- Screenshots of trading profits — these can be fabricated in seconds
- "Limited slots available" urgency pressure
- Requests payment via bank transfer, crypto or gift cards
- No FCA registration for anyone giving financial advice in the UK
- Course content is locked behind further paid upsells

Protection:
Check register.fca.org.uk before paying anyone for financial advice or signals.
No teenager genuinely making thousands per week from trading needs to sell
courses for 20 pounds. The course is the product — not the trading strategy.

UK reporting: FCA consumer helpline 0800 111 6768 or fca.org.uk/consumers


13. SKIN BETTING AND GAMBLING DISGUISED AS GAMING

THREAT LEVEL: MEDIUM-HIGH — unregulated, addictive, growing among 12-17s

What happens: Third-party websites allow players to use in-game cosmetic items
called skins — particularly from games like Counter-Strike — as betting chips
on simulated casino games or match outcomes. These items have real monetary
value, sometimes hundreds of pounds. Influencers and streamers promote big
wins on these sites, rarely disclosing they are paid to do so.

The sites operate outside UK Gambling Commission oversight. Young people lose
real-value items without the legal protections that apply to cash gambling.
The pattern also functions as a gateway to gambling addiction.

Warning signs:
- Websites offering to "double your skins" or enter items into a jackpot
- Streamers promoting third-party gambling sites with referral codes
- External sites asking for Steam or game login credentials
- Requests to deposit items before being able to withdraw winnings

Protection:
Never link your gaming account to a third-party site you have not verified.
If a website asks for your game login outside the official platform, close it.
Report illegal gambling sites to the Gambling Commission: gamblingcommission.gov.uk


14. SUBSCRIPTION TRAP SCAMS — FREE TRIAL TO HIDDEN RECURRING CHARGES

THREAT LEVEL: MEDIUM — affects anyone with access to a payment card

What happens: A free trial for a gaming boost, editing app, revision tool,
music service or beauty product requires card details to activate. Hidden within
terms and conditions is automatic conversion to a paid monthly subscription
unless cancelled within a short window — often 7 or 14 days. The charge begins
quietly, often described vaguely on bank statements, and continues for months
before the victim notices.

National Trading Standards research found 38% of UK adults had signed up to
a subscription without realising it, and nearly 5 million people were still
paying for subscriptions they had not knowingly agreed to as of late 2025.

Why young people are particularly affected:
- Many use parents' cards, Apple Pay or Google Pay without realising the
  continuous payment authority being established
- Small recurring amounts (5 to 15 pounds per month) go unnoticed
- App stores make in-app subscriptions particularly easy to trigger accidentally

Warning signs:
- "Free today" but asks for full card details
- Cancellation process requires a phone call, specific email or live chat only
- Company name on the bank statement differs from the app or website name
- Trial period is shorter than the billing cycle
- Pre-ticked checkbox agreeing to recurring payments

Protection:
Check bank statements weekly. Contact your bank immediately if an unauthorised
recurring payment appears — you can request a chargeback within 120 days.
Report to Citizens Advice on 0808 223 1133 or Trading Standards.


15. PARCEL DELIVERY SMISHING — MISSED DELIVERY TEXT SCAMS

THREAT LEVEL: HIGH — one of the most reported scam types across all UK ages

What happens: An SMS or WhatsApp message arrives claiming to be from Royal Mail,
Evri, DPD or another courier. It states a parcel could not be delivered and
requests a small redelivery fee of 1 to 3 pounds. The link goes to a
convincingly designed fake page that captures full card details. The small
fee is irrelevant — the card details are the target.

Young people are targeted because they are constantly shopping online and
regularly expecting parcels. The scam exploits the entirely plausible scenario
of a missed delivery.

Warning signs:
- Text arrives from a random mobile number rather than an official short code
- Sender name uses slightly wrong spelling (Royal-Mail, Evri-UK, DPD-Help)
- Link does not go to royalmail.com, evri.com or the carrier's official domain
- Urgent language — "respond within 24 hours or parcel returned"
- Asks for card details to pay a fee of just 1 to 3 pounds

Protection:
Never click delivery links in texts. Go directly to the courier's official
website and enter your tracking number there. Royal Mail and all major UK
carriers NEVER send payment requests via SMS.
Report scam texts free by forwarding to 7726 on any UK network.


16. EVENT AND CONCERT TICKET SCAMS

THREAT LEVEL: HIGH — losses more than doubled year on year in 2024

What happens: With sold-out concerts for major artists driving intense demand,
scammers create fake resale listings on social media — particularly Snapchat,
Instagram and Facebook Marketplace. They pose as someone who can no longer
attend, offer tickets below face value, collect payment via bank transfer and
disappear. The victim never receives tickets.

More than 1.6 million pounds was lost to gig ticket scams in 2024 alone —
more than double the previous year. People in their twenties are the most
targeted age group, accounting for 27% of all victims. Average loss per victim
is 150 pounds.

Warning signs:
- "Selling my ticket, can't go anymore" with no verifiable seller history
- Payment requested via bank transfer only — no PayPal, no card
- Refuses to use Ticketmaster, Viagogo or official resale platforms
- Cannot provide a photo of the physical or digital ticket before payment
- Price is suspiciously below the current resale market value
- Profile was created recently or has minimal history

Protection:
Only buy tickets through official ticket sellers or FCA-recognised resale
platforms. Never pay by bank transfer to an individual for a ticket.
If scammed, report to Action Fraud on 0300 123 2040 immediately.


17. ACCOUNT RECOVERY AND FAKE SUPPORT AGENT SCAMS

THREAT LEVEL: HIGH — growing rapidly on Discord, Instagram and Snapchat

What happens: A victim posts asking for help recovering a lost or locked account,
or receives a message warning that their account is at risk. A fake support
agent replies, often mimicking the official look of the platform. They ask for
a verification code sent to the victim's phone or email, claiming it is needed
to confirm identity. The code is actually the two-factor authentication code
for the account. Once provided, the attacker has full access.

This scam is common across Discord, Instagram and Snapchat. Young people use
these platforms intensively and often contact each other for technical help,
creating a natural environment for the scam to operate.

Warning signs:
- Support agent contacts YOU without being requested
- Account looks official but email address or username is not the platform's
  actual support address
- Asks for a 6-digit code sent to your phone or email
- Claims urgency — your account will be deleted if you do not act now
- Asks you to click a link to confirm identity

Protection:
No legitimate platform will contact you asking for a verification code.
If you receive a code you did not request, it means someone is trying to
access your account. Do not share it with anyone. Real support is accessed
only through the platform's official app settings.


18. DIGITAL GIFT CARD SCAMS

THREAT LEVEL: MEDIUM — used as payment method across multiple scam types

What happens: Scammers across several different fraud types — gaming scams,
sextortion, fake emergencies and impersonation calls — request payment via
digital gift cards rather than bank transfer. The most commonly requested are
Apple iTunes, PlayStation Store, Google Play and Steam cards. Gift cards are
nearly untraceable and irreversible, making them ideal for criminals.

The request for gift cards is itself a red flag for any transaction. No
legitimate company, government body, family member in genuine need or employer
ever requires payment in gift cards.

Warning signs:
- Any request for payment via gift card codes
- Being asked to scratch off the back and read out or photograph the code
- Request comes alongside urgency, threats or emotional pressure
- Person claims to be HMRC, a bank, a police officer or a family member
- Online friend or romantic contact asks for gift cards as a favour

Protection:
Hang up or stop messaging immediately. Gift card payment is the single clearest
signal that something is a scam — it does not matter what the stated reason is.
Report to Action Fraud: 0300 123 2040.


19. AI DEEPFAKE AND VOICE CLONING SCAMS — EMERGING THREAT 2025-2026

THREAT LEVEL: MEDIUM AND RISING — technology improving rapidly

What happens: Criminals use AI tools to clone a friend's voice from social
media audio or video, then send voice notes asking for money for an emergency.
A separate variant creates fake video of known influencers or peers appearing
to endorse fraudulent products or make requests. These are convincing enough
to fool close friends.

As of 2026 this technology is rapidly becoming accessible to amateur criminals,
not just sophisticated operators. Young people are at particular risk because
their voices and faces are abundantly available on TikTok, Instagram and YouTube.

Warning signs:
- Voice note sounds slightly robotic, flat or has unnatural pauses
- Emotional request for money arrives unexpectedly with urgency
- Request feels out of character for the person it claims to be
- Person cannot or will not respond to a live video call
- Message arrives via a new number or different account than usual

Protection:
Establish a safe word with close friends and family to verify identity in
emergencies. Always call the person back on their known number before sending
money. Hang up and call the person directly if a voice message asks for money.


20. FRIEND ACCOUNT TAKEOVER SCAM

THREAT LEVEL: HIGH — exploits trust, hardest scam for young people to detect

What happens: A friend's Instagram, WhatsApp or Snapchat account is hacked.
The attacker then messages the friend's contacts pretending to be them,
describing an emergency — stuck somewhere, phone about to die, needs money
urgently, will pay back immediately. Because the message comes from a real
known contact's account, the recipient trusts it instinctively.

This scam is particularly effective against young people because digital
communication is their primary means of contact with friends, and an urgent
message from a friend's account is indistinguishable from a real message
without verification.

Warning signs:
- Sudden emergency message from a friend asking for money
- Writing style feels slightly different — more formal or more urgent than usual
- Refuses or is unable to call or video call to explain the situation
- Asks for payment via bank transfer, gift cards or crypto rather than normal means
- Has not posted anything recent or seems unusually quiet despite the emergency

Protection:
Before sending any money, call the friend on their actual phone number — not
through the messaging app. If they genuinely need help they can speak to you.
Message another mutual friend to check if the story is true.
If a friend's account has been hacked, report it to the platform immediately.
